Three "Head Spear" Roles at the Federal Level

Translated from the 44-page strategic summary — connecting your core focus on education and technology to federal policy leadership.

Strategic Intent

These three roles represent the sharpest points of strategic leverage — areas where your background in education and technology most directly intersects with federal-level priorities, creating opportunities to lead rather than advise.

The Three Roles

1
Federal Education Technology Architect
Department of Education / OSTP — Policy & Innovation Lead

The claim: Position yourself as the architect of next-generation federal EdTech infrastructure — the person who bridges the gap between what education technology can do and what federal policy currently allows.

Why you own this: Most federal education officials understand policy but not technology. Most technologists understand systems but not pedagogy or procurement. You sit at the intersection.

  • Lead the federal definition of "digital equity" in practice, not just in policy documents
  • Shape how AI tools are evaluated, approved, and deployed in K–12 and higher education settings
  • Build the framework for federal EdTech standards that states must meet to receive funding

Entry point: Office of Educational Technology (OET) advisory role → federal rulemaking input → appointed position.

2
National Digital Workforce Strategy Lead
Department of Labor / Commerce — Future of Work Initiative

The claim: Own the federal narrative around preparing the American workforce for technological disruption — specifically the 60 million workers whose jobs will be redefined by AI and automation in the next decade.

Why you own this: This role requires simultaneous fluency in labor markets, technology adoption curves, and education pipeline design. It is not a policy role — it is a systems design role that requires exactly your combination of expertise.

  • Design the federal retraining architecture: who qualifies, what programs count, how outcomes are measured
  • Set standards for what a "technology-ready" credential means at federal level
  • Build public-private partnerships between community colleges, tech companies, and federal agencies

Entry point: Workforce Innovation and Opportunity Act (WIOA) advisory board → Commerce Department technology working group → Senate-confirmed director-level appointment.

3
Federal AI Literacy & Ethics Commissioner
FTC / OSTP / National AI Initiative — Public Trust Lead

The claim: As AI becomes embedded in education, hiring, housing, and healthcare, someone must be the federal voice ensuring the public understands and can critically engage with these systems. This is a new role that does not yet fully exist — you can define it.

Why you own this: AI ethics without education expertise produces compliance theater. Education reform without AI fluency produces outdated curricula. The synthesis is rare and federally underrepresented.

  • Define national AI literacy standards for K–12 and adult education
  • Lead federal guidance on algorithmic transparency in public-sector AI systems
  • Serve as the public-facing communicator bridging technical AI policy and civic understanding

Entry point: National AI Initiative Office contributor → FTC technology fellow → appointed Commissioner or Senior Advisor to the President on AI in Education.

Connecting Thread

All three roles share the same underlying logic: federal systems are making high-stakes technology decisions without sufficient educational perspective, and educational institutions are making high-stakes technology adoptions without sufficient federal coordination. You are positioned to close both gaps simultaneously.

Federal Strategy Overview

This document outlines the strategic positioning and objectives for federal-level engagement. It serves as the governing framework connecting all other strategic documents in this hub.

Strategic Pillars

1. Establish Credibility Through Specificity

Federal influence is earned through demonstrated expertise in a narrow area, then expanded. The strategy is to become the definitive voice on the education-technology nexus at the federal level before broadening to adjacent policy areas.

2. Build Institutional Relationships Before Appointments

Advisory roles, working groups, and federal task forces are the primary pathway to appointed positions. The strategy prioritizes relationship-building with agency officials, Senate committee staff, and White House policy offices over public-facing advocacy alone.

3. Create the Category, Then Lead It

The most durable federal roles are ones that did not exist before the person who claimed them defined their scope. The three Head Spear roles in this document set represent areas where no clear federal owner currently exists.

Key Federal Entry Points

  • Office of Educational Technology (OET) — Department of Education advisory and rulemaking body
  • National AI Initiative Office (NAIIO) — Cross-agency AI coordination under OSTP
  • Workforce Innovation and Opportunity Act (WIOA) — Federal workforce development funding and policy
  • Federal Trade Commission (FTC) — Technology regulation and consumer protection in digital markets
  • Senate HELP Committee — Health, Education, Labor, and Pensions — primary Senate jurisdiction

Education & Technology Focus Areas

Core competency document covering the intersection of education policy and technological innovation. This is the foundational expertise that underpins all three strategic federal roles.

Why This Intersection Matters Federally

The United States is experiencing simultaneous crises in education outcomes and technology workforce readiness. Federal agencies are responding in silos — the Department of Education focuses on pedagogy and access, the Department of Commerce focuses on technology competitiveness, and the Department of Labor focuses on workforce outcomes. None owns the intersection.

The Core Insight

Technology is reshaping what education must accomplish. Education is the only lever that can ensure technology serves everyone. Federal policy has not yet caught up to either reality. This gap is the strategic opportunity.

Education Policy Positions

Digital Equity as Infrastructure

Broadband access, device access, and digital literacy are not supplemental educational resources — they are foundational infrastructure equivalent to electricity and plumbing. Federal funding frameworks must reflect this.

AI in the Classroom: A Framework for Decisions

School districts need a federal framework for evaluating, procuring, and deploying AI tools — one that addresses data privacy, algorithmic bias, pedagogical effectiveness, and teacher training simultaneously. No such framework currently exists at federal level.

Credential Reform

The four-year degree as the default signal for workforce readiness is increasingly misaligned with employer needs and learner circumstances. Federal recognition of alternative credentials — micro-credentials, stackable certificates, competency-based assessments — requires a consistent federal quality framework.

Technology Policy Positions

Responsible AI Deployment in Public Systems

When AI systems are used in public education — for grading, discipline, admissions, or instruction — they are de facto public infrastructure and should be subject to transparency, auditability, and equity requirements.

Technology Procurement Reform

Federal and state education technology procurement is broken. Long contracts, vendor lock-in, and inadequate evaluation criteria produce expensive systems that fail students. Reform requires both policy changes and technical standards.
